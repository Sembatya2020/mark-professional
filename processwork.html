Processwork

What it is, and how it connects or differs from other ways

Process Oriented Psychology

My Introduction to Process Work

I have been practicing Process Work since 1991, when I began to study with Dr Arnold Mindell, Jean-Claude and Arlene Audergon in the United Kingdom. Together with them, I helped to establish the Research Society for Process Oriented Psychology, known as RSPOPUK. I qualified as a Diplomate in 1996 and have been registered with the UKCP since then. My longer-term teachers and supervisors have included Gary Reiss, Dawn Menken, Julie Diamond, and Max Schupbach.

Process Work is a non-pathological, nature-oriented, and awareness-based approach — and fundamentally, it is a heartful approach to human and non-human experience.

What is Process Work?

Identity and Identifications

Human beings have a deep tendency to identify in particular ways. We might identify as roles — parent, professional, partner — or we might identify with personality traits: I am confident, I am shy, I am creative, I am logical. We identify as genders, with capabilities, with woundedness or strength. Some of these identifications are conscious and deliberate. Others are entirely unconscious — patterns we live out without noticing them.

Many of these identifications are organized by society, culture, family, and lived experience. If we have experienced being disempowered or unheard, we may unconsciously develop identifications that compensate — we might become the strong one, the caretaker, the person who takes up space and asserts themselves. We become attached to these ways of being; they feel like who we are.

But these identifications are not only in the mind. They are held throughout the body — in posture, in movement, in the quality of our voice, in how we dress and present ourselves. They show up in our communication style, in the force of our presence, in the signals we send without speaking. A person who identifies as confident carries themselves differently than someone who identifies as uncertain. The identification is not abstract; it is lived.

Secondary Processes and Edges

But here is where Process Work begins its real inquiry. Alongside the identifications we carry consciously, there are always other aspects of experience, other qualities and potentials, that we do not identify with. These are what we call secondary processes — the things that are outside the circle of our identity.

In the most extreme forms, we project these secondary aspects away from ourselves entirely. We see them as belonging to someone else, to the world around us, to nature, to animals, to those we are in conflict with. This projection of disowned parts is a great source of conflict. When we believe we are separate from what we disown, we create the conditions for struggle — in relationships, in groups, in society, in our relationship with the more-than-human world.

At the boundary between our identified self and the non-identified aspects lies what we call the edge. An edge is a conscious or unconscious belief system that holds our identity in place. It is like the skin of our selfhood — it contains us and separates us from what we consider not-us.

These edges are constructed from many things. They include verbal beliefs and stories — statements we remember being told, negative messages from parents or teachers, conclusions we drew about who we are and what is possible for us. But edges are also held somatically, in the body — as patterns of tension, as held breath, as ways of tensing or recoiling when we approach certain experiences. Body memory is an essential part of how identity is maintained.

Following Process: Feedback and Awareness

The heart of Process Work is learning to follow experience as it unfolds, moment by moment. This is guided by something called feedback.

Feedback is not simply agreement or disagreement. It is an energetic response — a signal that someone, or a group, or a relationship is responding positively or negatively to the direction of travel. Someone might say "yes, I agree" while simultaneously holding tension, hesitating, or showing a micro-expression of discomfort. This is negative feedback in another channel. Real consent and ethical work require attending to feedback across all the ways human beings communicate — not just words.

The Process Work facilitator's role is to notice this feedback and to be led by it. This does not mean passivity or absence of direction. It means following the energy, following what wants to happen, following the process itself as it emerges. The facilitator becomes a channel for awareness and presence, noticing what is trying to come through.

This mirrors what we see in natural systems. Feedback is what guides ecological systems, weather systems, the body's own healing. When we ignore feedback persistently, it amplifies — it returns to us in other forms, often with greater force. When we pay attention to feedback, we move with life itself rather than against it.

Channels: Multiple Ways of Knowing

Process Work operates through what we call channels — the sensory and relational pathways through which experience flows.

The primary channels include sight, hearing, movement, proprioception (our sense of the body in space), and somatic experience. But there are also composite channels: the relationship channel, through which information flows between people; the world or environmental channel, through which we sense and are shaped by the more-than-human world around us.

Because Process Work recognizes that experience flows through all of these channels, a single session can move fluidly between many modalities. We might work with words and dialogue, exploring stories and meanings. We might move into the body — tracking sensations, following movement impulses, working somatically. We might work with sound, with visual symbols, with creative expression, with art. We might work in relationship, attending to what arises between people. We might work at the level of dreaming, of imagery, of the imaginal realm.

This multi-channel approach means that Process Work can meet people wherever they are, whatever is alive and trying to come through in that moment.

The Levels of Reality

Process Work recognizes that experience unfolds across three distinct yet interwoven levels of reality, each illuminated by awareness in the moment.

Consensus Reality is the level we know most intimately. Here, we experience ourselves as separate individuals with distinct identities, roles, and positions. In consensus reality, there are clear boundaries — I am this and not that. You are you and I am me. We and them. This level of reality organizes our social structures, our legal systems, our rituals, our codes of conduct and ethics. It defines the norms and moral expectations within families, cultures, and societies.

The advantage of consensus reality is enormous — it allows coordination, shared understanding, and collective function. The danger is that consensus reality can become too narrow. It can exclude and marginalize. When a culture privileges rational thinking and dismisses the imaginal, the intuitive, the dreaming — entire ways of knowing become invisible and disvalued. When one group's consensus reality is imposed as the reality, other ways of being are rendered invalid and wrong. Arnold Mindell observed that the marginalization of dreaming may be one of the most serious forms of oppression, visible historically in how indigenous cultures and their knowledge systems have been dismissed and destroyed.

Dreamland, or the dreaming level of reality, is more fluid. Here, there are still differentiated aspects — dream figures with distinct qualities, environments, atmospheres — but the boundaries are permeable. One can shift identity, morph, transform. Things that are rigid in consensus reality can flow and change.

In Process Work, dreaming is not confined to nighttime dreams. It comes through in body sensations and symptoms that don't fit consensus reality explanations. It arrives as unexpected hesitations or strange sounds in the voice. It shows up as synchronicities, as moments that seem to exceed rational explanation. It appears in the natural world — in animals, in weather, in the landscapes we move through. The dreaming level is always active; we are, in a sense, always dreaming. The work is to bring more awareness to the dreaming, to recognize its signals, and to allow its wisdom and creative potential into consciousness.

This is also where non-human forms of intelligence become visible. Consensus reality often privileges human, rational intelligence as the only "real" intelligence. But the dreaming level reveals that intelligence is everywhere — in animals, in ecosystems, in the patterns of nature itself. As we move toward more ecological and systems-based understanding, we begin to recognize that everything is interconnected through a complex dynamic of many forms of intelligence. There is no single true perspective or way of understanding; life itself, in its unfolding creativity, is the most fundamental intelligence.

Sentience — sometimes called essence — is the level before differentiation arises. It is pre-form, pre-part, a realm of pure quality before it has landed as a figure or taken shape. It is the emergence of something vital before it becomes articulated. This level is often beyond words; it is more a felt knowing, an intimation, a presence.

Working at the level of sentience can be deeply orienting. It offers access to something more fundamental than the parts and positions we usually inhabit. It can bring a quality of stillness or coherence that working at other levels alone cannot reach.

At the deepest level, underlying and present within all three realities, is what Arnold Mindell called Process Mind — something akin to the awareness that is present across and within all these levels of reality. This may be closest to what non-dual spiritual traditions point toward: not another level so much as the ground in which all levels arise and are held.

A Note on This Page

This page offers an introduction to Process Oriented Psychology as I understand and practice it. Process Work is vast — it has applications in individual therapy, couple and family work, organizational development, conflict resolution, and community facilitation. It weaves together Jungian depth psychology, somatic awareness, indigenous wisdom, systems thinking, and a commitment to awareness and presence moment by moment.
